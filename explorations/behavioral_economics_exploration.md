## 1. FOUNDATIONAL CONCEPTS

### Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979)

**Core Principles:**
- Reference dependence: People evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point rather than absolute terms
- Loss aversion: Losses are psychologically twice as powerful as equivalent gains (ratio ~2.25:1)
- Two-stage decision process: Editing (simplification via mental shortcuts) and Evaluation (attitudes toward risk)

**Contemporary Validation:**
- 2022 global study showed 90% replication of core theoretical contrasts across countries
- Theory's robustness confirmed despite replication challenges in other behavioral economics findings

**Key Insight:** Prospect theory fundamentally challenged expected utility theory and launched behavioral economics as a field.

### Bounded Rationality (Herbert Simon)

**Core Concept:**
- Rationality is limited by cognitive capabilities, information access, time constraints, and computational capacity
- People "satisfice" (satisfy + suffice) rather than maximize
- Decision-makers seek satisfactory solutions with available resources, not optimal ones

**Simon's Goal:**
Replace "global rationality of economic man" with rational behavior compatible with actual organism capabilities and environments.

**Recognition:** Nobel Prize in Economics (1978)

**Relevance to Groups:**
Sets foundation for understanding why collective intelligence can outperform individual experts—aggregation compensates for individual bounded rationality.

## 3. CORE PHENOMENA

### Heuristics and Biases

**Availability Heuristic:**
- Mental shortcut using immediately recalled examples
- Recent events disproportionately influence probability judgments
- Can lead to systematic overestimation of vivid/dramatic risks

**Representativeness Heuristic:**
- Judging based on similarity to stereotypes/prototypes
- Ignoring base rates and statistical reality
- Example: Assuming someone is a librarian because they're quiet, despite librarians being rare

**Anchoring:**
- Initial information (anchor) heavily influences subsequent judgments
- Persists even with irrelevant or arbitrary anchors
- Robust across domains and expertise levels

**Affect Heuristic:**
- Emotional responses guide decision-making
- "How do I feel about it?" substitutes for "What do I think about it?"

### Loss Aversion and Framing Effects

**Loss Aversion:**
- Prospective losses ~2.25x more impactful than equivalent gains
- Applies to information as well as material resources
- Creates status quo bias and endowment effects

**Framing Effects:**
- Same information presented differently produces different choices
- Loss framing often more effective than gain framing
- Example: "90% survival rate" vs "10% mortality rate"
- Loss-framed incentives increased take-up rates by 17% in field experiments

### Mental Accounting

**Key Features:**
- Money categorized into different mental "accounts"
- Violates fungibility principle
- Transaction utility vs acquisition utility
- People constrain spending from depleted mental budgets while other accounts have surplus

**Example:** Separate budgets for groceries vs restaurants, even though both draw from same income

### Temporal Discounting

**Hyperbolic Discounting:**
- Time-inconsistent preferences
- Steep discounting for near-term delays, shallow for distant delays
- Creates preference reversals over time
- "Present self" makes choices "future self" regrets

**Present Bias:**
- Overvaluing immediate rewards
- Underweighting long-term consequences
- Impacts health behaviors, savings, procrastination

### Sunk Cost Fallacy and Escalation of Commitment

**Sunk Cost Fallacy:**
- Continuing behavior due to past investments (time, money, effort)
- Irrecoverable costs influence future decisions
- Violates rational cost-benefit analysis

**Escalation of Commitment:**
- Increasing investment despite mounting evidence of failure
- Driven by confirmation bias, ego protection, desire to justify past decisions
- Common in organizational settings

### Overconfidence and Calibration

**Dunning-Kruger Effect:**
- Low-ability individuals overestimate their competence
- Pattern: worst performers show highest overestimation
- Mid-range performers moderately accurate
- Best performers slightly underestimate

**Calibration Issues:**
- Subjective probability judgments often poorly calibrated
- Confidence intervals too narrow
- Confirmation bias creates overly consistent evidence from memory

### Confirmation Bias and Motivated Reasoning

**Confirmation Bias:**
- Seeking, interpreting, remembering information confirming existing beliefs
- Ignoring or dismissing contradictory evidence

**Motivated Reasoning:**
- 2024 research: Motivation affects reasoning through biased cognitive processes
- Accuracy motivation enhances appropriate strategies
- Directional motivation enhances strategies yielding desired conclusions
- Evidence across immigration, climate change, gun laws, racial discrimination

**Mitigation:**
- Awareness activates analytical reasoning
- Seeking diverse perspectives
- Consciously engaging with conflicting evidence

## 4. CHOICE ARCHITECTURE AND NUDGES

### Nudge Theory (Thaler & Sunstein, 2008)

**Definition:**
Any aspect of choice architecture that alters behavior predictably without forbidding options or significantly changing economic incentives.

**Philosophy:** "Libertarian paternalism" - guide toward beneficial choices while preserving freedom

**Applications:**

1. **Retirement Savings:**
   - Automatic enrollment
   - Automatic escalation
   - Default investment vehicles
   - Dramatically increased participation rates

2. **Healthcare:**
   - Organ donation defaults
   - Opt-out vs opt-in policies
   - Medication adherence reminders

3. **Education:**
   - Healthy food at eye level in cafeterias
   - Default course selections
   - Simplified financial aid applications

4. **Environmental:**
   - Default green energy options
   - Social comparison feedback on energy use

### Government Nudge Units

**Global Adoption:**
- UK Behavioural Insights Team (2010) - first "Nudge Unit"
- Australia: NSW (2012), BETA federal (2016)
- 200+ nudge units worldwide as of recent count

**Policy Impact:**
- UK pension auto-enrollment substantially increased retirement savings
- Low-cost interventions
- Behavioral science fundamental to consumer protection, health care, environmental policy, tax policy

### Default Effects

**Organ Donation:**
- Opt-out countries: >90% registration rates
- Opt-in countries: <15% registration rates
- 6x higher registration in opt-out systems
- However: Complex effects on living donor rates (may decrease)

**Status Quo Bias:**
- People conform to defaults
- Inertia in decision-making
- "Choice" of not choosing

**Limitations:**
- Meta-analysis found no evidence of nudge effectiveness after correcting publication bias
- Context-dependent effectiveness
- Not a policy "silver bullet"

## 5. BEHAVIORAL GAME THEORY

### Deviations from Classical Game Theory

**Key Differences:**
- Players consider others' payoffs (social preferences)
- Limited strategic thinking
- Context effects matter
- Learning and adaptation

### Ultimatum Game

**Setup:**
- Proposer splits money with responder
- Responder accepts or rejects (both get nothing if rejected)

**Classical Prediction:** Proposer offers minimum, responder accepts

**Actual Behavior:**
- Modal offers around 40-50% (close to equal split)
- Offers <20% frequently rejected
- Negative reciprocity: willing to sacrifice payoff to punish unfair behavior
- Fairness concerns trump monetary maximization

### Public Goods Games

**Pattern:**
- Initial cooperation
- Gradual defection without punishment mechanism
- High cooperation when punishment available (despite cost to punisher)
- Unfairness exposure reduces subsequent cooperation

**Insight:** Fairness critically shapes cooperative behavior in social dilemmas

## 6. SOCIAL INFLUENCES

### Social Proof and Conformity

**Definition:** Copying others' actions in ambiguous situations

**Mechanisms:**
- Informational influence: assume others have better information
- Normative influence: pressure to fit in
- Uncertainty amplifies effect

**Outcomes:**
- Public compliance (outward conformity)
- Private acceptance (genuine belief change)
- Can promote positive behaviors or problematic herd behavior

### Herding Behavior

**Financial Markets:**
- Investors imitate others
- Can cause market inefficiencies, bubbles, crashes
- Keynes: "contagious animal spirits"

**Psychological Drivers:**
- Uncertainty
- Incomplete information
- Bandwagon effect
- Fear of missing out

**Relevance to Collective Intelligence:**
- Undermines independence assumption
- Can amplify errors rather than cancel them
- Information cascades override private information

## 7. COLLECTIVE DECISION-MAKING AND WISDOM OF CROWDS

### Core Concepts

**Wisdom of Crowds:**
- Collective opinion of diverse, independent individuals
- Aggregation without deliberation yields superior judgments
- Eliminates individual noise through averaging

**Diversity Prediction Theorem (Scott Page):**

**Formula:** Collective Error = Average Individual Error - Predictive Diversity

**Meaning:**
- Group accuracy improves with individual accuracy
- Group accuracy ALSO improves with diversity of perspectives
- Diverse errors tend to cancel out

**Conditions (James Surowiecki):**
1. Diversity of opinion
2. Independence of members
3. Decentralization
4. Effective aggregation mechanism

### Limitations and Challenges

**When Crowds Fail:**

1. **Correlated Biases:**
   - Similar systematic errors don't cancel
   - Opinion leaders spread idiosyncratic biases
   - Consensus bias from shared knowledge emphasis

2. **Social Influence:**
   - Undermines independence
   - Information cascades
   - Herding behavior
   - Average becomes less accurate (though median/geometric mean more robust)

3. **Groupthink:**
   - Deliberation can reduce diversity
   - Pressure for consensus
   - Free-rider problems

4. **Digital Crowd Issues:**
   - Demographic biases
   - Highly active user influence
   - Bot presence

**Research Finding:**
Social influence causes crowd average inaccuracy, but geometric mean and median remain robust aggregation methods.

### Prediction Markets

**Mechanism:**
- Financial stakes in outcome predictions
- Market price aggregates distributed information
- Dynamic continuous updating

**Advantages:**
- Incentive alignment (money at stake)
- Superior aggregation methods
- Handles complex information integration
- Outperforms expert analysis in many domains

**Challenges:**
- Market prices as social proof (double-edged)
- Potential for speculative bubbles
- Limited liquidity in some markets

**Key Insight:**
Collective intelligence superiority lies largely in aggregation methods, not just response quality.

## 9. CRITICISMS AND REPLICATION CRISIS

### Replication Failures

**Major Findings:**
- 2015 "many labs" study: >50% of psychological effects failed to replicate
- Loss aversion on "shaky ground"
- Identifiable Victim Effect failed replication (Jan 2024)
- Entitlement effect in Ultimatum Game: no replication in 1,250+ participants

**Publication Bias:**
- When corrected for publication bias, no evidence for nudge effectiveness remained
- Positive results preferentially published
- File drawer problem

**Fragility:**
- Different estimation procedures yield conflicting results
- Data preprocessing choices matter
- Econometric results sensitive to methodology

### Context Sensitivity

**Hidden Moderators:**
- Effects vary across contexts
- Original vs replication setting differences
- Cultural variations
- Temporal changes

**Implication:**
Behavioral effects may be real but more context-dependent than initially believed.

### Ongoing Debates

**Questions:**
- Which effects are robust?
- What are boundary conditions?
- How to improve research practices?
- Balance between discovery and replication

**Opportunities:**
- Improved methodology
- Larger samples
- Pre-registration
- Open science practices
- More careful theorizing about boundary conditions

## CONCLUSION

Behavioral economics reveals systematic patterns in how humans deviate from perfect rationality. These deviations are predictable, context-dependent, and often amenable to intervention through choice architecture.

For collective intelligence, the key insight is nuanced: individual irrationality doesn't necessarily produce collective irrationality when:
- Independence is preserved
- Diversity of perspectives exists
- Effective aggregation methods are used
- Appropriate incentives align behavior with information revelation

However, behavioral biases can also amplify in groups through social influence, herding, and shared systematic errors.

The field is currently navigating a replication crisis that is refining understanding of which effects are robust and under what conditions. Meanwhile, new frontiers are opening with AI integration, creating both opportunities (bias detection at scale) and risks (bias amplification through algorithms).

The connection to Joshua's work on collective intelligence and decision-making is profound: understanding individual behavioral patterns is essential for designing systems that harness collective wisdom while mitigating collective folly.
